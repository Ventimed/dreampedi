**CHAPTER 1**\n\n**INTRODUCTION TO SHORT EXAM**\n\nIt is the one form among clinical oral exams (i.e: long cases, short cases and viva). The short-exam section in pediatrics covers a number of systems (most commonly HEENT, respiratory, cardiovascular, abdomen, lower motor) and many miscellaneous cases such as: dehydration, malnutrition, rickets, anemia, Down syndrome, burn, edema, shock, skin lesion and so on.\n\nIn most of medical and health science schools in Ethiopia, the candidate will be examined mostly after long case examination often in the morning time. There will be one or more short cases based on the examiner. The candidate is examined by two examiners (one senior and one resident). Each short exam usually takes 15 minutes.\n\nShort cases tend to have a lead-in that is more directed, so a more focused examination is in order; the examiner may say: do chest examination, assess for signs of dehydration (DHN), assess for signs of edema …. Proficiency in short cases is dependent upon how much you practice preferably on a daily basis on real patients i.e “practice, practice, practice… makes perfect!” Several examination problems require a great deal of clinical material to be covered in 15 minutes; so a well prepared routine is essential. This book gives just an outline of an approach to most of the commonly seen short-case topics.\n\nOften the examiners will instruct that you may either talk as you proceed or examine in silence and summarise at the completion of the examination. Most candidates prefer the former method as it allows the examiners to see and hear how you think and should a questionable finding be noted; the examiners may guide you at that point. The examiner wants to see how you get the finding and also how you analyze it not on what you get.\n\nDo not wait for order from the examiner for each step of an examination and do not say “I would now like to look at … or now I am going to do …” just*** ***do it! Be confident but don’t be argumentative. If you become angry with an uncooperative child, you may fail; if you inadvertently hurt a child, such as in a joint examination, you may well fail and also inappropriate techniques of abdominal examination may lead to life threatening conditions like splenic rupture. If you do not follow the instructions of the provided introduction, you will fail; so keep your boundary of level of confidence and answer what you are asked. \n\nAt the completion of your examination, you may mention further areas that you would like to examine next and ask if time permits and then present a succinct summary of your findings with the most likely diagnosis, followed by a logically presented differential diagnosis. The differential diagnosis need not be exhaustive. In particular, it should never be a rote-learnt list for a certain type of case, but it should be relevant only to the particular child you have just examined. Be careful that the first diseases you mention are not inappropriate suggestions. Beware of ‘automatic list’ responses.\n\nAvoid terms like “I think perhaps…” you need to consciously tighten up your description of the patient’s finding you get. The examiner judges your: level of confidence, the way of approach to the patient, technique and steps of examination, timing, ability to interpret physical findings and case based discussion of investigations.  \n\nRemember, before starting to examine: communicate to the patient and attendant. Give a greeting and introduce yourself (act as a physician). Explain for the patient and attendant what you are going to do and ask for permission to proceed.\n\n**CHAPTER 2 - VITAL SIGNS**\n\n**Pulse Rate **\n\nThe radial pulse is used commonly to count the pressure readings and too large a cuff yields spuriously low pressure reading\n\nSteps in measuring blood pressure\n\nThe patient should avoid smoking or drinking caffeinated beverages for 30 minutes before the blood pressure is taken and rest for at least 5 minutes\n\nthe examining room should be quiet and comfortably warm\n\n Make sure the arm selected is free of clothing\n\nPalpate the brachial artery to confirm that it has a viable pulse\n\nPosition the arm so that the brachial artery, at the antecubital crease, is the heart level \n\nCenter the inflatable bladder over the brachial artery\n\nThe lower border of the cuff should be 2.5cm above the antecubital crease\n\nPosition the patient’s arm so that it is slightly flexed at the elbow\n\nTo determine how high to the cuff pressure; feel the radial artery with the fingers of one hand and rapidly inflate the cuff until the radial pulse disappears. Read this pressure on the manometer and add 30 mmHg \n\nPlace the bell of stethoscope lightly over the brachial artery, taking care to make an air seal with its full rim. Because the sounds to be heard (Korotkoff sounds) are relatively low in pitch, they are heard better with the bell\n\nInflate the cuff rapidly to the level just determined and then deflate it slowly, at a rate of about 2 to 3 mmHg per second. Note the level at which you hear the sounds of at least two consecutive beats. This is the systolic pressure.\n\nContinue to lower the pressure slowly. The disappearance point, usually only a few mmHg below the muffling point, is the best estimate of diastolic pressure\n\nBlood pressure can also be measured at lower extremities (popliteal artery), in cases of when we have too large cuff size that unfit to the upper arm and do not have another appropriate cuff size. After the age of infancy, systolic and diastolic blood pressure in the legs are 20 and 10 mmHg higher than the arms respectively. \n\nFour extremity blood pressure measurement as an initial screening is recommended to pick some abnormalities like coarctation of aorta and takayasu syndrome \n\nDuring inspiration, both the systolic and diastolic blood pressure decreased due to pooling of blood in the pulmonary bed \n\nMeasuring blood pressure while the child is crying can increase the blood pressure falsely by 40-50mmHg from the baseline\n\nBlood pressure interpretation according to the 2017 AHA definition updates:\n\nNormal blood pressure \t- BP between  5thand 90thcentile for age and sex\n\nElevated blood pressure - BP ≥ 90th  to < 95thcentile for age and sex\n\nStage I hypertension  - BP ≥ 95th to (95thcentile** +** 11 mmHg)\n\nStage II hypertension \t- BP ≥ (95thcentile  **+** 12mmHg)\n\nIf the systolic and diastolic blood pressure interpretations fall in different stages, take the severe one to stage. E.g. if systolic blood pressure is classified as elevated blood pressure and  diastolic blood pressure classified as stage II hypertension  , our final interpretation is the patient has stage II hypertension (since we take the severe one).\n\n**CHAPTER 3**\n\n**GENERAL APPEARANCE**\n\nPossible questions by the examiner\n\nWhat do you see?\n\nPossible differential diagnosis\n\nEvidence for
